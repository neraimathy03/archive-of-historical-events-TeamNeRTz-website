World War I (1914-1918)

World War I, fought from 1914 to 1918, was a cataclysmic conflict that reshaped the global political landscape and had profound social, economic, and cultural consequences. The war was triggered by the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary on June 28, 1914, which set off a chain reaction of alliances and hostilities. The major powers of Europe were drawn into the conflict: the Allies, primarily France, Russia, and the United Kingdom, were pitted against the Central Powers, mainly Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire. The war saw unprecedented levels of carnage and destruction, largely due to the introduction of modern military technology, such as machine guns, tanks, chemical weapons, and airplanes, and the extensive use of trench warfare on the Western Front, which led to a deadly stalemate.

The war's vast scale and brutality resulted in the mobilization of entire societies, with economies and populations heavily directed towards the war effort. The conflict profoundly affected the home front, causing significant social changes, including the increased participation of women in the workforce and the beginnings of shifts in class structures. The war ended on November 11, 1918, with the signing of the Armistice, but it left a legacy of deep political and social upheaval. The Treaty of Versailles in 1919 imposed harsh penalties on Germany, contributing to economic hardship and political instability, which eventually paved the way for World War II. World War I also led to the dismantling of empires, including the Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian, and German Empires, and the redrawing of national boundaries, creating a host of new nations and laying the groundwork for many of the geopolitical conflicts of the 20th century.
