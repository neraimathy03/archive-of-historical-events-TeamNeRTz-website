Beginning of the Islamic Calendar (622)

The beginning of the Islamic calendar, known as the Hijri calendar, marks the year of the Hijra in 622 AD, when Prophet Muhammad and his followers migrated from Mecca to Medina. This event was pivotal in Islamic history as it signified the establishment of the first Muslim community. Facing severe persecution from the Quraysh tribe in Mecca, Muhammad sought a safe haven where his followers could freely practice their faith. The journey to Medina, approximately 320 kilometers away, was fraught with danger but ultimately successful. Upon arrival, Muhammad was welcomed by the local tribes, and the Muslim community, or ummah, was established. The Hijra was so significant that it became the starting point of the Islamic calendar, with the first year being 1 AH (Anno Hegirae). In Medina, Muhammad laid the foundations for governance with the Constitution of Medina, outlining the rights and duties of all residents, Muslim and non-Muslim alike. This migration not only consolidated the teachings of Islam but also set a precedent for the concept of an Islamic state. The Hijra is commemorated annually by Muslims during the Islamic New Year, serving as a reminder of the early sacrifices and the establishment of Islam as a structured and community-oriented faith. The Hijri calendar is still used today to determine the dates of important Islamic holidays, such as Ramadan, Hajj, and Eid.
